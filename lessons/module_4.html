Prokaryotes vs Eukaryotes (Key Concepts)
  • Most living things you know such as animals and plants are multicellular organisms.
  • Some living things are made up of only single cell.
  • Single-celled or unicellular organisms include the bacteria, some protists, and some fungi.
  • Even though composed of single cells, these organisms carry out all the functions necessary for life.
  • In different organisms, cells also vary in sizes, shapes, parts, and functions.
  • But they all have one thing in common: they make up all living things and they are living.

What’s New?

As you move into Module 4: Prokaryotic vs Eukaryotic Cells, it is important to visualize the structural differences between these two types of organisms.

While most living things you are familiar with, like plants and animals, are multicellular eukaryotes, others—such as bacteria—are single-celled prokaryotes. The primary difference lies in the presence of a defined nucleus; eukaryotic cells have a nucleus with a visible membrane, whereas prokaryotic cells do not.

What is it?

The distinction between prokaryotes and eukaryotes is the most significant division in biology:

  • Prokaryotes ("Before the Kernel"): These are single-celled organisms that lack a membrane-bound nucleus and other specialized organelles like mitochondria. Their name is derived from the Greek words pro ("before") and karyon ("nut or kernel"), referring to the absence of a defined nucleus.
  • Eukaryotes: These organisms possess cells with a membrane-bound nucleus that protects their genetic material (DNA) within a nuclear envelope. They also contain complex organelles, such as:
    • Mitochondria: Found in all eukaryotes.
    • Golgi Apparatus: Responsible for processing proteins.
    • Chloroplasts: Present specifically in photosynthetic eukaryotes like plants and algae.
    • Variety: Eukaryotes can be unicellular (like protozoa) or multicellular (like plants, animals, and fungi).
