The cell is the smallest functional unit capable of life.

Scientific Context

Classical cell6 theory has three main pillars: (1) all organisms consist of one or more cells, (2) the cell is the basic unit of structure/function, and (3) all cells come from pre-existing cells. Option C is a general biological concept but not an official "postulate" of the theory.

Scientific Context

In 1665, Robert Hooke used a primitive microscope to look at cork and described tiny, box-like compartments he called "cells," comparing them to the small rooms inhabited by monks.

Scientific Context

Often called the "command center," the nucleus houses the cell's DNA. It uses this genetic information to direct essential activities like growth, metabolism, and reproduction.

Scientific Context

The mitochondrion is the site of cellular respiration, where nutrients like glucose are broken down to produce ATP (energy). This energy powers nearly all cellular functions.

Scientific Context

Epithelial tissue forms the protective outer layer of the skin (epidermis) and lines the internal surfaces of organs like the digestive tract. It serves as a barrier and helps with absorption.

Scientific Context

Mitosis is the process of cell division used for growth and repair. It ensures that each new daughter cell receives a full, identical set of chromosomes from the parent.

Scientific Context

Facilitated diffusion is a passive process (no energy required) where transmembrane proteins, such as carrier proteins, help specific molecules cross the membrane by moving them from high to low concentration.

Scientific Context

Both Diffusion and Osmosis are examples of passive transport where molecules move "downhill" toward a lower concentration. Osmosis is specifically the diffusion of water.

Scientific Context

Active transport requires the cell to expend energy (usually ATP) to move substances "uphill" against their natural concentration gradient. This is essential for accumulating high concentrations of needed molecules like ions and glucose.
